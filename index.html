1. Executive Summary

In 2025, global green energy reached a significant milestone as renewables surpassed coal to become the largest source of electricity generation worldwide. Clean power, including renewables and nuclear, now generates approximately 43% of the world's electricity, with record solar growth being a key driver.

2. Comparison Data

According to Ember's Global Electricity Review 2025, clean power sources reached a 40.9% share of global electricity generation in 2024, with renewables like solar and wind overtaking hydropower. This review forecasts that clean power will exceed 43% of the global electricity mix by the first half of 2025.

3. Regional Performance Analysis

In 2025, the global green energy transition shows a shift toward China and emerging economies, with China accounting for roughly 60% of global renewable capacity growth and India on track to become the second-largest growth market. Policy changes have significantly altered the United States' growth trajectory, while the European Union saw solar become its largest power source for the first time.

4. Section Specific Drivers

According to Ember, key drivers of green energy demand include the growth of AI and data centers, increasing cooling needs due to extreme heatwaves, and the expansion of the "Electrotech" sector encompassing solar, wind, batteries, and electric vehicles. Ember also notes that climate-driven demand contributed to a rise in fossil fuel generation in 2024 because clean energy could not always meet sudden spikes in demand.

5. Critical Barriers to Growth

  1. Grid and Infrastructure Bottlenecks

    The most critical barrier is the global "interconnection queue."

    • Grid Congestion: Over 3,000 GW of renewable projects are currently waiting for grid connections worldwide.
    • Expansion Needs: To meet 2030 targets, the global grid must expand by roughly 2 million km annually. In the U.S., 70% of energy firms cite grid limitations as their primary obstacle to growth.
    • Curtailment: Due to insufficient transmission, "curtailment" (wasted clean energy) is rising significantly in markets like the UK, where wind curtailment reached 8.5% in 2024.

  2. Policy and Regulatory Volatility

    Frequent shifts in government frameworks create a high-risk environment for long-term investment.

    • U.S. Policy Shifts: New legislation in 2025 (such as the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") has cut U.S. renewable forecasts by nearly 50% due to the early phase-out of tax credits and new land-use restrictions.
    • Permitting Delays: Lengthy siting and permitting processes remain a top barrier in Europe, India, and the U.S., often leading to project cancellations due to community opposition or bureaucratic fragmentation.

6. Progress towards 2030 Targets

In early 2026, progress toward the 2030 green energy targets presents a "two-speed" reality: global deployment is breaking records, yet national policy and infrastructure are struggling to keep pace with the massive scale required.

  1. Renewable Capacity: The Tripling Goal
    • The Deployment Boom: Between 2023 and 2025, renewable additions grew by an average of 29% annually, outperforming the 21% growth rate originally needed. Because of this early surge, annual additions now only need to grow by 12% per year from 2026 to 2030 to stay on track.
  2. Energy Efficiency: The Doubling Goal
    • Current Rate: In 2024, global energy intensity improved by only 1%, far below the required 4% benchmark.
    • Investment Need: To realign with 2030 targets, annual investment in efficiency must increase sevenfold to approximately $2.2 trillion.
